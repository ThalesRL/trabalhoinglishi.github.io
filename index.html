History

Northern Ireland was created in 1921, when Ireland was partitioned by the Government of Ireland Act 1920, creating a devolved government for the six northeastern counties. As was intended by unionists and their supporters in Westminster, Northern Ireland had a unionist majority, who wanted to remain in the United Kingdom, they were generally the Protestant descendants of colonists from Britain. Meanwhile, the majority in Southern Ireland (which became the Irish Free State in 1922), and a significant minority in Northern Ireland, were Irish nationalists (generally Catholics) who wanted a united independent Ireland. Today, the former generally see themselves as British and the latter generally see themselves as Irish, while a Northern Irish or Ulster identity is claimed by a significant minority from all backgrounds.

Coin

Sterling is the currency of the United Kingdom and nine of its associated territories. The pound (sign: £) is the main unit of sterling, and the word pound is also used to refer to the British currency generally, often qualified in international contexts as the British pound or the pound sterling.

Location

Northern Ireland, a constituent country of United Kingdom situated in the northeast of the island of Ireland. Wich possess green landscapes and dramatic coastlines, Northern Ireland's beauty is both serene and inspiring. The Giant's Causeway stands as a testament to its geological wonders, while the Mourne Mountains offer breathtaking views that have inspired poets and artists for generations.

Accents

Accents in Northern Ireland vary from region to region, each carrying its own distinctive charm and cadence. From the lyrical lilt of Derry to the hearty brogue of Belfast, the diversity of accents mirrors the diversity of its people.

Languages

English is the predominant language spoken in Northern Ireland, but Irish (Gaeilge) holds a special place as a minority language, cherished by communities striving to preserve their linguistic heritage.

Summary
